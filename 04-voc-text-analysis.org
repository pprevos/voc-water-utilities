While human readers excel at interpreting the nuances and context of language, the sheer volume of modern customer feedback presents a significant challenge. Computational methods allow us to scale our understanding, provided we first transform natural language into a structured, numeric format suitable for statistical analysis.

When analysing technical water measurements, data structures are usually well-defined time series. We measure something (pressure, flow, quality) at a defined location and time. Time series data is as such relatively easier to analyse and understand than a messy collection of text files. Natural language data comes to us in many different forms, from streams of short social media messages to massive slabs of text from call transcripts, interviews and so on. To analyse this type of data we need to convert it to a standardised numeric representation that allows a computer to extract meaning.

This chapter discusses how to convert a text corpus (a collection of texts) into a format that can be computationally analysed through tokenisation, a Document-Term Matrix and vectorisation techniques. These methods form the foundation of all Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, including Large Language Models. This chapter forms the bases for the next two chapters that discuss sentiment and topic analysis. This chapter contains a small case study applying these methods to a transcript of an interview with a customer service professional.

* Storing text as Data
When analysing a large volume of text we need to decide on our unit of analysis. Textual data exists at different levels and a hierarchy of the units of analysis exists [cite:@stoltz_2024_txt]:

- /Corpus/: A collection of texts, for example a collection of transcripts of customer phone calls or an extract of social media posts.
- /Document/: The main unit of observation such as a single transcript or a social media post.
- /Chunk/: A section of a document, that consists of one or more paragraphs or sentences (bigrams, trigrams).
- /N-gram/: Two or more words.
- /Word/: A single word or acronym (also called a unigram).
- /Character/: One or more letters in alphabetical scripts (such as English, Cyrillic, or Greek), a syllable in syllabaries (e.g. Japanese or Cherokee), or a word in logographic scripts such as Chinese.

In computational text analysis, the workflow usually starts with a corpus of text. We want to know something about this corpus. For example, the case study to the next chapter uses a corpus of tweets about text water and we like to know how people feel about this service. Each tweet in this corpus is a document, which can be examined individually.

In most applications each document splits into analysable elements (tokens), usually /n/-grams or unigrams. For more fine-grained applications, such as Large Language Models, texts split into in groups of characters, also called shingles. Tokenised texts are a good starting point to understand the content of a corpus. For more complex applications we need a Document-Term Matrix, which summarises a corpus in a single matrix suitable for statistical analysis. With the advent of deep learning and Large Language Models we can also convert a text to numeric vectors, which leverages the complexity of this technology by embedding meaning in to our numeric representation.

* Tokenisation
The first step in most text analysis projects is to split the text into tokens. Tokenisation is the act of  splitting human-readable text into the units of analysis. The text below is an extract from a simulated conversation between a market researcher and a water utility customer:

#+begin_quote
Honestly, I don’t really think about the water until there’s a problem. I guess my confidence is mixed? Most of the time it looks perfectly clear, but sometimes, especially in the summer, it has a really strong chlorine smell, almost like a swimming pool. Because of that, my family actually uses a filter for all our drinking water to remove the smell. I trust it enough to shower and cook with, but I wouldn't just chug a glass straight from the bathroom sink because I don't like smelling chlorinated water.
#+end_quote

Tokenisation interprets a text as a 'Bag of Words'. This approach treats a document as an unordered collection ("bag") of unigrams, ignoring grammar, syntax, and word order [cite:@stoltz_2024_txt]. For example, tokenising the customer quote above into a Bag of Words typically involves counting the occurrence of each word: "water" and "smell" appear twice, and "chlorine" appears once, and so on.

While this method is computationally efficient and conceptually simple, the primary limitation of the Bag of Words model is the loss of semantic context because his approach ignores the word order. The model for "the water is bad, not good" is identical to "the water is good, not bad", even though these phrases have diverging meanings. Despite this limitation, this model remains a highly effective foundation for basic frequency, sentiment, and topic analyses.

Before tokenising a text we need some pre-processing. The first step in most tokenisation workflows is to convert the text to lowercase and remove punctuation except for hyphens and apostrophes. Some applications also remove numbers and redundant whitespace. The level of pre-processing depends on the purpose of the analysis.  In some more advanced applications, exclamation and questions marks are retained. When analysing social media we also need to deal with emoticons and hashtag symbols.

We can tokenise the example text into individual words and count the frequency of each word, as shown in table [[tab-unigrams]] below. The first obvious observation is that this tokenisation is not informative. The top five words consist of articles, a pronoun and a conjunction, which linguists call /function words/. These are words that have little or no lexical meaning and express grammatical relationships within a sentence. In contrast, /content words/ possess semantic content and express the meaning of the sentence in which they occur.

Text analysis software includes capabilities to remove function words from the corpus to focus on the meaning of the text. Data scientists call these stopwords because they are removed or 'stopped' before the analysis commences. Analysis workflows contains predefined lexicons of stopwords, but analysts can also define project-specific stopwords. For example, in a text about a water utility, the word "water" is uninformative. While water is the core subject of the text, removing it from the text prevents it from compressing the rest of the data, allowing more important topics such as pressure or billing to surface.

After removing standard stopwords, our top-five list of worlds (with ties listed alphabetically) looks like the one in table [[tab-no-stopw]].

Stopping words from being analysed needs to be handled with care. If the main research question is to determine the sentiment of a text then it is best not to remove any words. When the main research problem is to determine the topics of a corpus, then some words are best removed so that the analysis can focus on words that express meaning.

Single-word tokenisation can obscure the meaning of a text because is erases the relationship between words. We can partially rectify this problem by using /n/-grams. Table [[tab-bigrams]] shows the first five bigrams (sequences of two words) of the sample text. In this example, bigrams such as  "strong chlorine" and "chlorine smell" preserve meaning otherwise lost in the single-word tokenisation. In this small example, using bigrams is not the most effective tokenisation strategy because each bigram only appears once.

  bigrams <- answer |>

    mutate(token = 1:n()) |>
    select(Token = token, Bigram = word)

  head(bigrams, n = 5)

Using /n/-grams splits a text in groups of two (bigram) or more words (trigram and so on). This method preserves some of the text structure but comes at a cost as the number of unique tokens dramatically increases because /n/-grams overlap. While the example text has 66 unique words, the bigram model has 89 unique terms. The /n/-gram model enhances semantic analysis but it comes at a cost as it increases the computational effort to analyse data. while bigrams increase dimensionality, they are often the only way to catch differences between, for example, "not good" and "good".

* Stemming and Lemmatisation
:NOTES:
- [X] [[https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/stemming][What Is Stemming? | IBM]]
- [X] [[https://cloud.google.com/discover/what-is-stemming][What is stemming and how does it work? | Google Cloud]]
:END:
Before tokenisation we use pre-processing techniques such as converting all text to lowercase, removing stopwords, numbers, whitespace and punctuation. These techniques normalise the text and reduce the model size, but we are still faced with some of the vagaries of natural language.

In alphabetic languages the same word can appear in numerous inflected forms. People might use words such as  "connection", "connections", "connected", "connecting", or "connect" when applying to become a new tap water customer. This variety of words can be problematic when using a literal search. Searching for "connected" would only find one of these instances. Using different versions of effectively the same word also increases the data model. Two further techniques that are available to reduce the dimensionality of a text are stemming and lemmatisation.

Stemming simplifies inflected words and replaces them with their stem or root form. In this case, all five words stem to "connect". Various stemming algorithms are available for different languages[cite: @pant_2025_stem;@singh_2016_text;]. They use rules to transform a text, for example stripping suffixes, such as "-en", '-ing", "-ion", and "-ly". 

#+begin_src R :results none :eval no :tangle no
  # Example of stemming using the Snowball algorithm
  library(SnowballC)

  wordStem(c( "connection", "connections", "connected", "connecting", "connect"), language = "english")
#+end_src

The algorithmic approach used in stemming is not entirely accurate because human spelling is logically inconsistent. Stemming struggles with irregular verbs, such as forms of "to be". The rule-based approach of stemming can lead to terms that are not real words.

Lemmatisation is a linguistically more accurate technique that includes the larger context of each word. Lemmatisation uses dictionaries and detects parts of speech, to distinguish between verbs, nouns and other word types. However, lemmatisation is a lot more computationally intensive approach and might not be necessary for business applications, such as {{{voc}}} research.

When stemming the example text from the previous section with the [[https://snowballstem.org][Snowball string processing library]] [cite:@porter_2001_snow], we see that the stem of "chlorine" and "chlorinated" becomes the non-existing word "chlorin", which isn't a word but serves as a mathematical token that captures the shared meaning of "chlorine" and "chlorinated". The benefit of this stemmed term is that Instead of two words with a count of one each, we have one term with a count of two, highlighting the importance of chlorine on the taste experience.

#+begin_src R :colnames yes :tangle no
  unigrams_stemmed <- answer |>
    unnest_tokens(word, text) |>
    anti_join(stop_words, by = "word") |>
    filter(word != "water") |>
    mutate(word = SnowballC::wordStem(word)) |>
    count(word, name = "Count") |>
    arrange(desc(Count)) |>
    select(Word = word, Count)

  head(unigrams_stemmed, n = 5)
#+end_src

* Document-Term Matrix
:NOTES:
- Concept: Explain that rows = documents and columns = unique words (terms).
- Sparsity: Mention that most cells will be zero (since one customer doesn't use every word in the English language).
- Weighting (TF-IDF): Briefly introduce Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency. It’s the "gold standard" for making sure common words like "water" (in a water utility corpus) don't drown out specific issues like "chlorine."
:END:
After tokenising and cleaning a text, the next step is to create a Document-Term Matrix or DTM, which is a numeric representation of the Bag-of-Words of our data. Converting a text to a matrix opens a lot of opportunities to use linear algebra to analyse a text which is the foundation of all machine learning [cite:@atteveldt_2021_comp].

To create a DTM from the example text used in this chapter, we need to split it into five sentences, so that each sentence becomes a 'document', or row in the matrix. Each unique term in the text is a column and the numbers represent how often each word appears in each document. The first seven columns of the DTM for the example paragraph looks like table [[tab-dtm]]. We see here, for example, that the term "chlorin" appears once in sentence 3 and 5.

#+begin_src R :colnames yes :tangle no
  # Create Document-Term Matrix
  sentences <- answer |>
    unnest_tokens(sentence, text, token = "sentences") |>
    mutate(document = paste("Sentence", row_number()))

  dtm <- sentences |>
    unnest_tokens(word, sentence) |>
    anti_join(stop_words, by = "word") |>
    filter(word != "water") |>
    mutate(word = SnowballC::wordStem(word)) |>
    count(document, word) |>
    cast_dtm(document, word, n)

  dtm_matrix <- as.matrix(dtm)

  # displayfirst seven columns
  bind_cols(data.frame(Sentence = 1:nrow(dtm_matrix)),
            as.data.frame(dtm_matrix[, 1:7]))
#+end_src

A typical feature of a Document-Term Matrix is its sparsity. Most cells contain a zero because a single document (in this case, a sentence) only uses a fraction of the vocabulary present in the corpus. In real-world applications with thousands of documents, these gigantic matrices can contain over 99% zeros. 

While counting raw frequencies is a logical starting point, it treats all words with equal importance. As we saw in the tokenisation examples, common words like "water" or function words will dominate the matrix, potentially drowning out specific, meaningful issues like "chlorine" or "filter". To resolve this and give more weight to words that distinguish documents from one another, data scientists use a weighting technique called Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF).

While raw frequency counts (Term Frequency, or TF) are useful, they can over-represent words that appear frequently across all documents. In a water utility corpus, the word "billing" might appear in every document, making it useless for distinguishing between a document about "leaks" and one about "metering".

Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) solves this by multiplying TF by a second metric:
- Term Frequency (TF): How often a word appears in a specific document.
- Inverse Document Frequency (IDF): A measure of how rare a word is across the entire corpus.

When analysing interactions between a customer and an interviewer or an agent, the language patterns used by these two will vary greatly. The customer service agent of interviewer uses standardised language, while the customer speaks from their own lived experience. 

By using TF-IDF, a word like "chlorine" that appears multiple times in a single transcript but rarely in others will receive a high weight, signaling its importance as a specific customer concern.

The TF-IDF score for a term $t$ in a document $d$ is calculated as:

$$W_{t,d} = tf_{t, d} \times \log \big( \frac{N}{df_t} \big)$$
Where $N$ is the number of documents in the corpus, $tf_t, d$ the frequency of term $t$ in document $d$ and $df_t$ the number of documents containing term $t$. This approach ensures that the analysis focuses on the distinctive vocabulary of the customer's feedback.

This approach is further develop in the case study at the end of this chapter.

* Embeddings
Another method to convert a text to a series of numbers are Large Language Model (LLM) text embeddings.  A Large Language Model cannot not only be used as a chatbot but we can also use the embeddings to codify our texts.

The training process for LLMs starts with a Document-Term Matrix (DTM) which is processed by the machine learning tool. The LLM algorithm detects patterns in the DTM and stores the text in a vector space. This means that each token is expressed as a coordinate in a metric space, where each dimension expresses a semantic value.

To illustrate this we can use a simple example with two dimensions (sewerage and water).

LLMs use thousands or even millions of dimensions, allowing for a fine-grained text model. In these cases we cannot simply apply a meaning to each dimension as in this example.

While a Document-Term Matrix (DTM) treats "smell" and "odour" as two entirely separate columns, vector embeddings recognise their semantic proximity. In a high-dimensional vector space, such as a LLM, these words are located close to one another.

This mathematical closeness allows us to perform vector arithmetic. For instance, in a well-trained model, the relationship might hold that:

$$Vector(Filtered) - Vector(Chlorine) \approx Vector(Pure)$$

This allows the analysis to move beyond keyword matching and into the realm of conceptual understanding.

* Exploratory Analysis Case Study
After converting a text into a table of word frequencies we can explore the content to glimpse a
first sense of its meaning. The case study used for the remainder of this chapter is an interview with a water utility professional, known as Mr. Pink  [cite:@prevos_2016_invis]. The text has been modified and enhanced with a Large Language Model to protect anonymity and demonstrate some principles of exploratory analysis in natural language processing. The R script for this analysis is available in the Git repository that accompanies this book.

#+begin_src R :results none 
  # Exploratory analysis of interview

  library(tidyverse)
  library(tidytext)
  library(SnowballC)

  # ETL
  raw_text <- read_lines("data/interview.txt")

  interview_df <- tibble(raw = raw_text) |>
    filter(str_detect(raw, "Pink|Interviewer")) |>
    extract(raw, 
            into = c("speaker", "text"), 
            regex = "^(?:\\\\s*)?(Interviewer|Mr Pink):\\s*(.*)",
            remove = TRUE) |>
    mutate(text = str_trim(text)) |>
    drop_na(speaker)

  # Tokenisation and word count
  word_counts <- interview_df |>
    unnest_tokens(word, text) |>
    anti_join(stop_words, by = "word") |>
    filter(word != "water") |>
    mutate(word = wordStem(word)) |>
    count(speaker, word) |>
    filter(!word %in% c("that’", "it’", "what’", "they’re", "question")) |>
    group_by(speaker) |>
    slice_max(order_by = n, n = 10, with_ties = FALSE) |>
    ungroup()
#+end_src

** Relationships Between Words
:NOTES:
- [ ] Term Co-occurrence Network
:END:

Beyond individual word frequencies, we often want to see which words "travel together." Term Co-occurrence Networks (or Bigram Networks) visualize the structure of a conversation by linking words that frequently appear in the same context.
